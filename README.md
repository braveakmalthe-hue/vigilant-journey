# North Korea’s History and Current Situation

North Korea (the Democratic People’s Republic of Korea, or DPRK) is a **secretive, authoritarian state** born from the Cold War division of Korea.  After Japan’s 1945 surrender in World War II, the peninsula was split at the 38th parallel: Soviet forces occupied the North and the Americans the South.  The North declared itself the DPRK on September 9, 1948, with Kim Il-sung (a former guerrilla leader favored by the Soviets) as premier【18†L384-L387】.  The Korean War (1950–53) entrenched this division; no peace treaty was signed, so technically the two Koreas remain at war.  Millions died in that conflict and afterwards both sides built heavy military defenses along the Demilitarized Zone (DMZ). Today North Korea remains isolated and heavily militarized, viewing South Korea (and by extension its allies) as enemies. 

## Political System and Ideology

North Korea is ruled by a **single party dictatorship** centered on the Kim family.  Kim Il-sung ruled from 1948 to 1994 with a cult of personality; his son Kim Jong-il took over until 2011; and now Kim Jong-un (the grandson) holds supreme power.  The regime combines a Stalinist-style command economy with *Juche* (self-reliance) ideology and a “military-first” policy.  In practice there are **no free elections or opposition parties**.  The government strictly controls all media, religion, and travel.  Critics describe it as one of the world’s most repressive societies.  For example, analysts note that Kim Jong-un “silenced all opposition by detaining between 80,000 and 120,000 political prisoners,” and routinely threatens neighboring countries with missile strikes【36†L88-L93】.  Any perceived dissent can lead to harsh punishment. 

- *Founding (1948):*  The DPRK was proclaimed at the first meeting of the Supreme People’s Assembly, with Kim Il-sung as premier【18†L384-L387】.  The Soviet Union immediately recognized the new state.  The North Korean constitution and institutions were modeled on Soviet lines, with a single “Workers’ Party” in control.  
- *Kim Dynasty:*  The regime’s *chuch’e* (Juche) ideology emphasizes loyalty to the leader and Korean self-reliance.  After Kim Il-sung’s death, his son became “Dear Leader,” and today Kim Jong-un is absolute ruler.  Loyalty to the family is enforced by strict propaganda.  (North Korean citizens are taught to regard the leaders as almost godlike.)  
- *Human Rights Abuses:*  Inside North Korea, the government imposes extreme controls.  All media are state-run and citizens grow up hearing only the state’s version of events.  Even minor acts like listening to foreign radio or expressing doubt about the regime are illegal.  The United Nations reports that the overall human-rights situation has *not improved* over the past decade and in many ways has worsened【25†L204-L212】. Political prison camps still operate; in them hundreds of thousands who “disappeared” (including foreign nationals) remain unaccounted for【25†L232-L240】.  Forced labor is common: for example, thousands of orphans and poor children are reportedly sent to work in dangerous mines or construction as unpaid “shock brigades”【25†L246-L252】.  In Islamic terms, this is especially painful to consider because Islam condemns oppression and teaches that every life is sacred. The Prophet ﷺ said cruelty and tyranny are forbidden, so reading these reports troubles us deeply. 

  - **Prison Camps and Repression:** There are reports of *political prisoner camps* holding tens of thousands in harsh conditions【25†L232-L240】. Families can be punished if even one member is accused of disloyalty. The UN has found that state surveillance and crackdowns are pervasive.  
  - **Propaganda and Isolation:** From birth, North Koreans are fed constant propaganda. The regime even updated laws to widen capital punishment for crimes like watching foreign TV shows. In fact, enjoying foreign media (films, music, etc.) can now carry the death penalty【25†L239-L244】. The authorities try to “block the people’s eyes and ears” as one escapee put it【25†L204-L212】, meaning all information is filtered.  
  - **Food and Welfare:** Publicly, the government claims to provide healthcare and education for all. In reality, resources are uneven.  A quarter of the population is estimated to need food assistance.  The UN found that the “right to food continues to be violated” in North Korea, and government policies (like prioritizing the military) actually make hunger worse【25†L233-L240】. During the COVID-19 pandemic, North Korea sealed its borders completely (one of the strictest lockdowns anywhere), cutting off most trade and aid【37†L153-L158】. This deepened shortages and suffering, especially since the regime itself provided no reliable health information or vaccines【37†L153-L158】.  

## Economy and Living Conditions

North Korea’s economy is **small and struggling**.  It is officially socialist and highly militarized, but in reality some market activity (black markets, private farming plots) has emerged.  The country publishes almost no reliable statistics; independent estimates suggest its GDP per capita is only a few hundred dollars (PPP), among the lowest in the world.  Sanctions over its nuclear program, along with mismanagement and natural disasters, have kept the economy weak. 

Recently there has been a slight rebound: South Korea’s central bank reported that North Korea’s GDP grew about 3.7% in 2024 (up from 3.1% in 2023), the fastest pace in eight years【24†L190-L198】. This growth is **heavily driven by Russia relations** – industrial and mining output surged because of new projects and arms exports to Russia in the Ukraine conflict【24†L202-L210】.  For example, the chemical sector expanded strongly to make metal products for weapons sent to Russia【24†L202-L210】.  But overall trade remains tiny: in 2024 total trade volume was only about $2.7 billion (South Korea’s is over $900 billion), with exports of just $360 million【24†L239-L247】.  The average North Korean’s income is roughly 1.7 million won (~$1,240) per year【24†L243-L247】, just a few percent of South Korea’s per capita income.

A Hyundai executive donated 501 cattle to help feed people during the devastating famine of the late 1990s【41†L130-L138】.*  Historically, North Korea has faced **famine and food crises**.  In the mid-1990s, the collapse of Soviet support and disastrous floods led to a famine in which perhaps 2–3 million people starved (often called the “Arduous March”).  Aid from China, South Korea and even the U.S. was sometimes allowed (as in the cattle donation shown above【41†L130-L138】), but the government still could not feed everyone.  Today the situation is not as extreme, but many families are weak. Markets exist in most towns, but prices are high and supply is uncertain. Officially, the government boasts of successes (like new housing blocks or factories), and indeed satellite images show ongoing construction in Pyongyang. In practice, rural areas suffer power cuts, poor healthcare, and food shortages. A UN press release in 2025 noted that North Korea in that decade pursued policies that increased *isolation*, leaving its people “under such restrictions [that] no other population in today’s world” experiences【25†L219-L222】. 

  - **Industry and Construction:** State-directed construction (e.g. monuments, apartment blocks) still occurs, as symbols of national pride. The Bank of Korea noted that manufacturing output rose in 2024 mainly due to closer ties with Russia【24†L202-L210】. Construction grew as well (plans for grand buildings and infrastructure continue), but these projects often consume resources without improving ordinary life.  
  - **Markets and Currency:** Since the 2000s a kind of market economy has taken root alongside the old system. Private trade (with Chinese or Russian intermediaries) is common, and many transactions now use foreign currency (dollars or yuan) despite official bans. Some analysts say “dollarization” is both a lifeline and a threat to government control.  
  - **Covid Lockdown:** By 2020, North Korea’s choice to keep borders closed meant almost no tourists, diplomats, or foreign business. Humanitarian groups couldn’t even bring in aid easily. This extreme isolation sent already weak GDP growth to near zero in 2020 and 2021【37†L153-L158】. Only in late 2022 did the government finally admit it had COVID outbreaks, but by then most foreign help had long been barred.  
  - **Statistics:** All data on North Korea’s economy come from outsiders (like South Korea’s unification ministry and bank) since Pyongyang does not release true figures. In 2024 the Bank of Korea’s estimate noted that mining grew 8.8% (biggest since 1999) due to coal and minerals trade【24†L203-L210】【24†L239-L247】, but even those gains are from a very low base. Overall, **poverty and hardship are widespread** by any measure. 

## Military and Nuclear Program

The DPRK is **militarily obsessed**. A large share of national resources goes to defense.  For decades North Korea has developed nuclear weapons and missiles to deter perceived enemies. In 2006 it conducted its first nuclear test; since then it has tested at least six devices, claiming even a hydrogen bomb by 2017【36†L117-L125】.  The regime frequently *announces* new missiles and displays them in parades. U.S. intelligence believes North Korea now has enough fissile material for **at least half a dozen nuclear bombs and possibly many more**【36†L108-L113】. It also possesses dozens of ballistic missiles of various ranges.

- **ICBMs and Threats:** In 2017–18 North Korea flew its first ICBMs (the Hwasong-14/15), which can reach Alaska and parts of the continental U.S.  Satellite images later showed preparations for more advanced missiles.  In late 2023 North Korea tested a new solid-fuel ICBM (Hwasong-18), which can be launched on shorter notice【37†L174-L182】.  The country’s official media often *names targets* of its weapons; analysts note North Korea has published maps showing strikes on U.S. bases in the Pacific, Guam, Hawaii, and even U.S. cities【29†L296-L304】. Such public threats are meant to deter enemies by fear.  It is believed, however, that like any nuclear state North Korea would only use such weapons if the regime’s survival were at stake.  
- **Missile Tests:** North Korea launches missiles routinely to demonstrate capability. After a two-year break, it began a rapid new tempo of tests in 2022–23.  For example, in November 2022 it fired a record 23 ballistic missiles in one day, including a missile that flew over Japan【37†L174-L182】.  In March 2026, during a major U.S.-South Korean drill called “Freedom Shield” (with 18,000 allied troops involved), the North responded by firing two missiles into the sea【46†L140-L144】.  These provocations are meant to signal defiance and to show that North Korea’s retaliation capability is real.  
- **Conventional Forces:** Besides nukes, North Korea maintains large ground, naval, and air forces. It has a massive tank force and thousands of artillery pieces (some within range of Seoul), though much equipment is outdated.  All young men must serve in the military (the service can last 10 years or more).  Militarily, North Korea is not stronger than the U.S.-South Korea alliance or the U.S.-Japan alliance, but it can inflict great damage given its proximity and numbers.  
- **Nuclear Command:** Notably, North Korea practices its command system. In April 2024 it reportedly tested its “nuclear trigger” system by simulating multiple rocket launches with dummy warheads – a drill to ensure the leader can issue orders in a crisis【29†L289-L297】.  This suggests a high priority on keeping the weapons ready.  

## International Relations and Impact

North Korea’s actions have **global repercussions**. Its nuclear ambitions make it a focus of international security discussions, while its isolation and abuses draw humanitarian concern. Here are some key points:

- **China – “Traditional Ally”:** China is North Korea’s main backer. Chinese leaders and North Korea’s say the two countries share a “tradition of friendship” that will endure no matter what【22†L109-L114】. China supplies most of North Korea’s oil, food, and trade. In September 2025, President Xi Jinping welcomed Kim Jong-un to Beijing and pledged to “consolidate and boost” ties【22†L109-L114】. Still, China is nervous about North Korea’s unpredictability. Beijing officially supports denuclearization, and Chinese analysts caution against readapting a formal three-way alliance with North Korea and Russia【22†L164-L172】. In practice, China uses its influence sparingly, balancing between stopping a war (which could send refugees into China) and not upsetting an embattled ally.  
- **Russia – Close Partners:** In recent years, North Korea and Russia have become unusually close. The Kim regime has sent weapons, ammunition, and reportedly troops to help Russia’s war in Ukraine. In return, Russia provides fuel, economic aid, and high-tech arms to North Korea【22†L122-L126】【24†L212-L216】. This “support-for-arms” swap has given Pyongyang some needed cash and materials, explaining the recent economic uptick【24†L202-L210】【24†L212-L216】. It also ties North Korea more openly to Russia’s geopolitical battles (making it harder for Western countries to ignore sanctions-busting). Russia now allows some trade with North Korea that was previously blocked. However, this reliance is double-edged: if Russia ever makes peace in Ukraine, North Korea could lose its lucrative market and political cover.  
- **United States and Allies:** The U.S. views North Korea as a nuclear threat that must be contained. America maintains ~28,500 troops in South Korea and supports advanced missile defense (like THAAD) on the peninsula【36†L128-L136】. Washington has led efforts to sanction North Korea under UN mandates. In 2017, the U.S. re-listed North Korea as a “state sponsor of terrorism,” tightening sanctions. Despite harsh pressure, the U.S. has also engaged in diplomacy: President Trump met Kim Jong-un three times (2018–2019), which halted tests briefly but achieved no lasting agreement. Under President Biden, the policy is “diplomacy and deterrence.” The U.S. says it is ready to talk “without preconditions”【37†L174-L182】, but demands nuclear disarmament. U.S. strategy also involves strengthening alliances: in 2023 President Biden, South Korean President Yoon, and Japanese Prime Minister Kishida announced a new trilateral security framework. These alliances aim to deter North Korea and reassure East Asian friends.  
- **South Korea (ROK):** South Korea is most directly affected by the North. The two Koreas have had periods of reconciliation (e.g. 2018 summits, joint statements to pursue denuclearization), but also periods of conflict. The current Seoul government under President Yoon Suk Yeol has taken a firmer stance. It has called for reform of inter-Korean engagement bodies and expanded U.S.-ROK military cooperation【37†L194-L202】. South Korea shares the threat perceptions of its allies.  Seoul also faces the challenge of North Korean defectors and cries for reunification. The **humanitarian impact** of North Korea’s isolation weighs on South Koreans’ conscience, but strategic thinking often dominates policy.  
- **Japan:** Japan regards North Korea as a grave threat (missile overflights and tests endanger its territory). Japan has joined all major sanctions and supports the U.S.-ROK alliance. It also presses on the abduction issue (Tokyo demands answers about Japanese citizens kidnapped by the North in the 1970s–80s).  
- **United Nations and World:** The UN has repeatedly condemned North Korea’s nuclear tests and human rights abuses. Since the first nuclear test in 2006, the Security Council passed **six major sanctions resolutions** banning weapons trade, capping fuel imports, freezing assets, etc. These have significantly crippled North Korea’s trade and finances, though enforcement is imperfect. Various countries (EU members, Canada, Australia, etc.) impose additional sanctions. On the humanitarian side, UN agencies sometimes provide limited food or medical aid to North Korea, but under strict state control. Most aid agencies say that because of the regime’s restrictions, much of the population still suffers in silence.  

**Global Impact:** North Korea remains a flashpoint in global politics. Its nuclear and missile programs force regional powers to spend on defense and complicate U.S.-China relations. Any conflict on the peninsula could draw in the U.S., China, Russia, Japan and others – all with different stakes. Moreover, North Korea has sold arms and missile technology to other pariah states (e.g. Iran, Syria, Myanmar), so its reach extends beyond Asia. Its very existence as an isolated nuclear state challenges international norms. 

For us who value justice and human dignity, North Korea is a tragic case: millions live under hardship and fear. Islam teaches that we must care for all people and speak against tyranny. The North Korean people – Muslims or not – deserve our prayers and hope.  We must also recognize that North Korea’s leaders **do hold real power** and bear responsibility for the state’s direction. From a moral perspective, tyranny (ظلم) is always condemned in Islam, and wealth is meant to be shared (as through zakat or charity). Reports of forced labor, torture, and starvation【25†L232-L240】【25†L246-L252】 deeply sadden the heart. At the same time, we recall that in Islam the blame rests on those who govern unjustly, not on innocent people. 

Ultimately, North Korea today is a **dynastic dictatorship with nuclear weapons**, locked in confrontation with much of the world. Its history of war and ideology has led it to emphasize security above all. Meanwhile its people suffer under scarcity and repression. Moving forward, the international community seeks ways to restrain North Korea’s weapons (through pressure and dialogue) and encourage humanitarian relief – all while avoiding war. Insha’Allah, peace and justice will prevail one day on the Korean peninsula.  Understanding these realities helps us remain compassionate toward the North Korean people, advocate for their well-being, and support efforts to resolve conflicts through diplomacy and fairness.
